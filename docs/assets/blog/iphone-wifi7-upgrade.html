TL;DR — a new iPhone + an old router = wasted speed

Apple’s latest iPhones ship with Wi-Fi 7 radios. The headline features—320 MHz channels and Multi-Link Operation (MLO)—unlock big jumps in real-world throughput and lower latency. But you only get those gains if your router also supports Wi-Fi 7 on the 6 GHz band. If you’re still on Wi-Fi 5 or Wi-Fi 6, your phone falls back and the “new phone, same old Wi-Fi” problem shows up immediately in backups, AirDrop, and streaming.

Bottom line: To feel a new iPhone’s Wi-Fi 7 speed, you need a Wi-Fi 7 router (with 6 GHz + 320 MHz) and decent placement/coverage. Otherwise, the phone is overqualified for your network.

What Wi-Fi 7 actually changes for iPhone

Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be) brings several improvements relevant to phones, not just laptops and consoles:

  • 320 MHz channels on 6 GHz: Doubles the maximum channel width versus Wi-Fi 6E (160 MHz). Wider lanes mean more data per second—great for big media and cloud work.
  • Multi-Link Operation (MLO): Lets one device use multiple bands/links in parallel (e.g., 6 GHz and 5 GHz) to increase throughput and resilience. If one link hits interference, the other keeps your flow smooth.
  • Higher-order modulation (4K-QAM): Packs more bits per symbol in clean conditions for higher peak rates.
  • Better scheduling & lower latency: Enhancements reduce contention and jitter—noticeable in live photos/video, gaming, and FaceTime.

These aren’t synthetic spec bumps—they’re features your iPhone can actually use at close/medium range on 6 GHz with a modern router.

Apple workflows that get faster (and feel better)

Here’s where Wi-Fi 7 helps with day-to-day Apple tasks:

  • AirDrop large files (ProRAW photos, ProRes video): Bigger channels + MLO reduce transfer times, so handing off 4K/8K clips doesn’t stall your day.
  • iCloud backups & restores: Nightly backups complete faster; device-to-device transfer on a fresh setup flies on a Wi-Fi 7 mesh.
  • Apple TV 4K & AirPlay: Higher throughput and steadier latency help 4K HDR streams and mirrored gameplay look/feel stable.
  • FaceTime & Apple Arcade: Lower jitter = fewer blips and smoother input response.
  • Photos/Video offloads to Mac via local network: Faster wireless imports when you’re near your router or a Wi-Fi 7 mesh node.

320 MHz & 6 GHz coverage realities

320 MHz lives on the 6 GHz band, which is fast but doesn’t travel through walls as well as 5 GHz. Expect the biggest wins in the same room or adjacent rooms. For whole-home benefits, consider:

  • Wi-Fi 7 mesh with a wired backhaul (ideal) or high-quality wireless backhaul.
  • Placement: Put the main router central and high; avoid cabinets and metal obstructions.
  • Channel selection & AFC (where applicable): Use auto channeling; keep 6 GHz enabled.

Upgrade checklist (5-minute audit)

  1. Check your router label/UI: If it doesn’t say “Wi-Fi 7” or “6 GHz,” it won’t deliver 320 MHz to your iPhone.
  2. SSID sanity: Make sure 6 GHz is enabled (some routers create a single “smart connect” SSID; others add a dedicated 6 GHz SSID).
  3. Ports matter: Look for at least one 2.5G port (WAN or LAN). If your ISP is multigig, 1G ports will cap you.
  4. Firmware: Update to the latest—vendors refine MLO/6 GHz performance post-launch.
  5. Mesh or single-node? If you have dead zones, plan for a Wi-Fi 7 mesh with wired backhaul where possible.

What to buy (specs that actually matter)

  • Certified Wi-Fi 7 with 6 GHz band enabled out of the box.
  • 320 MHz channel support (and puncturing) for the widest lanes your environment allows.
  • MLO for multi-band resilience and better aggregate throughput.
  • 2.5G (or 10G) WAN/LAN so your LAN doesn’t bottleneck a multigig ISP or local transfers.
  • Mesh-ready if you have >2,000 sq ft or multiple floors; prefer wired backhaul to keep 6 GHz clean for clients.
  • WPA3 security; avoid legacy WPA2-only deployments.
Don’t overspend on raw “theoretical” speed. For phones, coverage, latency, and stability beat peak lab numbers. A properly placed mid-range Wi-Fi 7 router can outperform a top-tier unit hidden in a closet.

Setup tips for iPhone owners

  • Keep 6 GHz on and allow band steering; iPhone will prefer 6 GHz when signal is clean.
  • Name your network once (single SSID) unless you’re troubleshooting—Apple devices handle steering well on modern routers.
  • Use WPA3-Personal; drop legacy b/g protection modes that slow airtime.
  • Place a mesh node in rooms where you shoot/edit video or back up photos—optimize for your workflow.
  • After upgrading, forget/rejoin your Wi-Fi on iPhone and run a quick speed/latency test in multiple rooms.

FAQ

Do I need multigig internet to benefit? No. Even with 500–1000 Mb/s service, Wi-Fi 7 improves latency and local transfers (AirDrop, NAS, Mac imports). Multigig WAN is a bonus, not a requirement.

Will my older devices still work? Yes—Wi-Fi is backward-compatible. They’ll connect at their best supported standard while your new iPhone uses Wi-Fi 7.

Is 320 MHz always active? It depends on interference and region rules. Your router chooses the cleanest channel width; in many homes you’ll still see big gains over 160 MHz.

Mesh or single router? If you have dead zones, go mesh (prefer wired backhaul). In smaller apartments, a single high-quality Wi-Fi 7 unit placed well is fine.
