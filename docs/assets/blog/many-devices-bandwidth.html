TL;DR — capacity is shared; manage airtime and uplink

  • Segment devices by band: Phones/laptops on 6 GHz; TVs/consoles on 5 GHz or wired; IoT on 2.4 GHz.
  • Turn on OFDMA/MU-MIMO & WPA3.
  • Enable QoS/SQM and cap bulk uploads to ~80–90% of upstream to prevent bufferbloat.
  • Use mesh with wired backhaul (or MoCA) in larger homes so nodes don’t steal client airtime.
  • One SSID with band steering; avoid random extenders.
Key idea: Wi-Fi performance is governed by airtime. Slow or chatty devices can consume disproportionate time, shrinking what’s left for everything else.

Airtime, not Mbps: how Wi-Fi really splits capacity

On a channel, clients take turns. A single slow device (weak signal, legacy standard, low-rate modulation) can occupy more time to send the same amount of data, throttling everyone else. That’s why separating device types and improving signal quality matters more than chasing theoretical peak Mbps.

The Wi-Fi 7 toolbox: MU-MIMO, OFDMA, MLO, 6 GHz

  • OFDMA: Splits a channel into sub-carriers so multiple clients can transmit simultaneously—great for many small packets (IoT, phones).
  • MU-MIMO: Spatial streams to serve multiple clients at once; helps when several high-throughput devices are active.
  • MLO (Multi-Link Operation): A single device uses multiple bands/links concurrently, smoothing bursts and reducing retries.
  • 6 GHz + 320 MHz (Wi-Fi 7): Big, clean lanes for modern clients = less airtime per transfer → lower contention.
Translation: Enable these features and keep modern clients on 6 GHz; their work finishes faster, leaving more airtime for everyone else.

Device mix: who should live on which band

BandPut these hereWhy
6 GHz Phones, laptops, tablets (Wi-Fi 6E/7) Highest capacity, lowest latency near nodes; frees 5 GHz for legacy/heavier clients.
5 GHz TVs, consoles, older laptops Good reach + throughput. Wire if possible to avoid airtime use during binges.
2.4 GHz IoT (plugs, sensors), long-reach gadgets Best penetration; keep chatter away from fast bands. Use channels 1/6/11 only.

QoS & SQM: stopping bufferbloat

Congestion pain often comes from the uplink (your upload pipe). When one device saturates uploads (cloud backup, camera, phone sync), queueing delay explodes—calls sound robotic, games stutter. Fix it:

  1. Enable QoS/SQM on the router and set real ISP speeds (use off-peak speed test).
  2. Cap bulk uploads to ~80–90% of upstream.
  3. Prioritize work devices/apps (conferencing, real-time) via MAC or application profiles.

Mesh & backhaul: don’t starve clients

  • Wired backhaul (best): Ethernet or MoCA between nodes preserves wireless airtime for clients.
  • Wireless backhaul (good): Prefer tri/quad-band or Wi-Fi 7 with MLO so backhaul doesn’t collide with client traffic.
  • Placement: Nodes ~30–40 ft line-of-sight; reduce through heavy walls. Aim for −55 to −65 dBm between nodes.
Avoid: Random “extenders” that halve throughput and raise latency. Use proper mesh/APs integrated into one system.

15-minute congestion playbook

  1. Update firmware; enable WPA3, OFDMA, MU-MIMO, 6 GHz.
  2. One SSID (band steering on). Move modern clients to 6 GHz automatically.
  3. Wire the hogs: TV/console/NAS via Ethernet; if not possible, park them on 5 GHz.
  4. IoT segregation: Keep IoT on 2.4 GHz (or Guest/IoT SSID) with no LAN access.
  5. QoS/SQM: Set speeds, prioritize work devices, cap backup/sync apps.
  6. Backhaul: Wire mesh if you can; otherwise ensure a dedicated wireless backhaul and good node RSSI.
  7. Room tests: Run speed + latency in office, TV room, bedrooms; move nodes 5–10 ft and re-test.
